Opioid Deaths in Connecticut
A Data Analysis of an Epidemic

Analyzing the Opioid Problem


The State of Connecticut has been dealing with a drug abuse epidemic, that on average, it claims two lives every day from drug overdose.

From 2012 to 2018 Connecticut has experience 5,106 accidental deaths from drug abuse. The death toll has puzzled numerous members of the state and the nation, pressuring lawmakers to pass bills in effort to abate the crisis.

Understanding the Drugs

Prescription opioids - prescribed by doctors to treat moderate to severe pain. Common types of prescription opioids include: oxycodone (OxyContin), hydrocodone (Vicodin), morphine, and methadone.


Fentanyl - a synthetic opioid pain reliever. It 50-100 times more potent than opioids like morphine and is approved for treating severe pain, typically advanced cancer pain.


Heroin - an illegal opioid.

Opioid Prescriptions

After the increase of deaths by drug abuse the State of Connecticut began a Prescription Monitoring Program (PMP)in an attempt to control the problem. The plot below demonstrates how death rates have increased even after the founding the PMP. Citizens decreased the intake of prescription drug and turned to other form of opioids.

Death toll by Drug Consumption

The dataset demonstrates the progression from prescription opioid abuse to the utilization of Synthetic Opioids. After 2014, Synthetic drugs increased exponentially while Heroin and prescription drug rates slowly decreased. Results of the shift is due to Synthetic Opioids low dosage potency and lack of government tracking.

Who's being affected

Demographics of Victims

We examined state data on 4,507 opioid-related overdose deaths from 2012 through 2018 to see which groups were most affected. First, we looked at age and gender. The plots below illustrate that the victim demographic is extensively composed of males (75%) between the ages of 26-55 years old. Heroin and Synthetic Opioids account for the majority of opioid overdose deaths. Seventy-five percent of opioid deaths were individuals between the ages of 25 and 55. The largest age group 26-35 represented 27% of the total deaths.

Deaths by Gender and Race

In Connecticut, death by opioid overdose is more common among white people than among other racial and ethnic groups. The largest number of opioid deaths (80%) from our data were white, which is greater than the percentage of the white population in the state (67%). Other races/ethnic groups had a lower percentage of deaths by opioid overdose in relation to the proportion of race/ethnicity for the state. Twelve percent of opioid deaths were hispanic people compared to a 16% proportion of the state population. Seven percent were black, which is lower than the state demographic of 12%. (For our purposes, "white" and "black" include those identified in the data as being white alone or black alone, not Hispanic. Anyone described as Hispanic, regardless of race, was categorized as Hispanic).


Affected Areas

Deaths by City and Drugs

After looking at demographics, we wanted to see if there were any specific areas/locations that had higher rates of death. From the first chart, we can see the cities where the most deaths occurred. Thirteen percent of all deaths in Connecticut for this time period occurred in Hartford. The next three cities, Waterbury, New Haven, and Bridgeport each had 8% of total deaths.


Income distribution & Drug Usage

The map below shows the the number of deaths by city and drug category. You can see that the larger cities tend to have more deaths, but the problem is widespread throughout the state. While looking at the map, we added a layer for per capita income and noticed that the cities with the highest number of deaths also had lower per capita income.

Deaths by City & Unemployment Rate

The per capita income led us to take a look at the correlation between unemployment rates and number of deaths for cities. We ran a linear trend model of the average unemployment rate by city versus the number of deaths by city. The model generated a p-value less than 0.001 which suggests a strong linear relationship and indicates that cities with higher unemployment rates generally experienced higher casualties from opioids.

Rehabilitation Centers Distribution

We also wanted to find out if there is a correlation between number of deaths and locations of rehab centers, which is the map below. The State of Connecticut has rehabilitation centers spread out throughout the state but is the density and distribution of the centers enough to tackle the problem? From the map, there appear to be a greater number of rehab centers where the deaths are occurring, but we were not able to determine if the centers had any affect on the death rates.

Tackling the Problem

To abate the crisis, legal and holistic efforts have been implemented by the government and, in some cases, by private citizens. From new laws to increased education, a steady effort is undergoing throughout the state. To name a few:

  • Fining drug companies for failure to monitor opioid distribution properly
  • Improving prescription records
  • All public servants for emergency services should carry Narcan for emergency application
  • New laws have been passed to prevent, deter or palliate the crisis:
    • The “good Samaritan” law grants immunity from arrest, charge, or prosecution for drug possession when individuals who are experiencing or witnessing an overdose summon emergency services
    • Increase in criminal penalties for the sale of Fentanyl
  • Expanded rehabilitation centers.

Much has been done, but much more needs to be done. In order to apply resources more effectively, we applied a XGB (Xtreme Gradient Boost) classifier model to the data. The model allowed us to determine, in a scientific way, the most important features of our model; the ones with most impact on the output. In plain English, which are the most vulnerable populations to which resources need to be directed


XGB Classification Model


Long Short Term Memory Network (LSTM)


The LSTM was utilizes LSTM model to predict the death toll by drug in 2019 based on drug-related deaths from 2012 to 2018. LSTM utilizes a recurrent neural network to make predictions in a time series with more accuracy than ARIMA (plain simple regression) because it learns the non-linearity of the data.


The model helped us understand that even though prescription opioids related deaths are decreasing and heroin related deaths are NOT increasing at the same rate, unfortunately, the death toll continues to go up because of synthetic opioid that people originally hooked with prescriptions are now using.
